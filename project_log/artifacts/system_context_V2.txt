PROJECT CONTEXT — HTC AIRSHIP DISASTER RELIEF SYSTEM
Version 2.0 | Updated after scoping and analysis design session

PROJECT IDENTITY


Company Concept:
A disaster relief company specializing in airship-conveyed, modular
hydrothermal carbonization (HTC) reactor systems for on-site waste
valorization in disaster-affected zones.
Core Technology:
Hydrothermal Carbonization (HTC) — a thermochemical process that converts
wet organic waste into hydrochar, process water, and recoverable gases
under elevated temperature (180–250°C) and autogenous pressure (~10–40 bar).
HTC tolerates high-moisture feedstock without pre-drying, making it suited
to disaster waste streams.
Mission Niche:
Deploy rapidly to disaster zones and convert locally generated waste streams
into usable energy or sequestered carbon, without reliance on fixed
infrastructure. The system must be energy self-sufficient — capable of
powering its own operations from waste conversion outputs.
Deployment Architecture:
Modular reactor units are airship-conveyed and lowered to ground level for
operation. The number of modules per deployment is payload-constrained.
Hydrogen in the airship lifting bladder is a candidate supplemental fuel
source: as modules are deployed and the airship offloads mass, hydrogen
can be selectively vented and combusted to supplement reactor energy input.
This creates a buoyancy-energy tradeoff that must be explicitly modeled.

SYSTEM FRAMING


Primary Lens: Process-First
The system is defined by its process chain. Feedstock selection and
deployment scenarios are evaluated against process requirements.
Primary Sizing Constraint: Energy Self-Sufficiency
The system must generate enough recoverable energy from waste conversion
to power its own operations.
Energy Self-Sufficiency Condition:
Q_net(feedstock, moisture, throughput, T_reactor) >= W_pump + W_auxiliary
Where Q_net = Q_reaction - Q_loss - Q_preheat_net
And   Q_preheat_net = Q_preheat × (1 - ε)   [after heat recovery]
This inequality, evaluated across the feasible operating envelope, is the
central technical result of the project.
Waste Streams in Scope:

Municipal organic (food waste, sewage sludge)    [primary focus]
Agricultural residue (crop waste, animal manure)
Industrial organic (processing byproducts)
Petrochemical / mixed hydrocarbon waste



PROCESS CHAIN (5-STAGE MODEL)


Stage 1 — Intake & Preprocessing
Function:  Waste reception, sorting, size reduction, moisture conditioning
Key var:   Moisture content (fraction), particle size (mm)
Constraint: Energy cost of conditioning must not exceed feedstock energy value
Stage 2 — HTC Reactor
Function:  Thermochemical conversion at elevated temperature and pressure
Key var:   T_reactor (180–250°C), P_autogenous (~10–40 bar), residence time
Constraint: Must produce net positive Q_net for self-sufficiency
Mechanical: Reactor vessel is a pressure vessel; wall thickness governed
by hoop stress. This is a structural constraint on minimum
vessel mass, which feeds into payload analysis.
Stage 3 — Product Separation
Function:  Phase separation of hydrochar, process water, and gas
Key var:   Separation efficiency, parasitic electrical load
Constraint: Minimize energy draw; maximize char recovery
Stage 4 — Energy Recovery
Function:  Heat integration via counterflow heat exchanger; optional
power generation from process heat
Key var:   Heat exchanger effectiveness ε (dimensionless, 0–1)
Constraint: Recovered heat must reduce Q_preheat sufficiently to close
the energy balance
Stage 5 — Output Disposition
Function:  Hydrochar end-use, process water treatment, gas handling
Key var:   Char HHV (kJ/kg), carbon content (%), gas composition
Constraint: Output pathway (fuel vs. sequestration) determines optimization
objective — energy output or carbon credit


KEY ENGINEERING TENSIONS



Moisture Content vs. Energy Cost
HTC tolerates wet feedstock, but heating the water fraction is thermally
expensive (c_p_water = 4.18 kJ/kg·K). There is a crossover moisture
threshold above which Q_preheat exceeds Q_reaction, making the feedstock
an energy liability. This threshold is feedstock-specific and is the
primary output of the thermal balance module.
Throughput vs. Thermal Mass / Scale
Smaller modular reactors have higher surface-area-to-volume ratios and
lose proportionally more heat to surroundings. Self-sufficiency is harder
to achieve at smaller scales. The minimum viable throughput for
self-sufficiency is a key design output.
Hydrochar as Fuel vs. Sequestration
Combusting hydrochar to power the reactor trades long-term carbon
sequestration for operational sustainability. This is a strategic
decision that determines the system's optimization objective.
Airship Payload vs. System Scale
Airship payload capacity imposes a hard upper bound on the number of
reactor modules and their individual size. This is the outer constraint
that all engineering analysis must fit within.
Hydrogen Offtake vs. Buoyancy (NEW)
Airship lift is generated by hydrogen in the lifting envelope. As reactor
modules are lowered and mass is offloaded, the airship becomes more
buoyant. Venting hydrogen at this point to supplement reactor fuel is
operationally convenient — but each kg of hydrogen vented reduces lift
capacity for subsequent operations. The tradeoff: energy gained from
hydrogen combustion vs. lift capacity sacrificed. This must be modeled
as a coupled buoyancy-energy calculation.
H2 LHV ≈ 120 MJ/kg. Lift per kg H2 = (ρ_air - ρ_H2) × g ≈ 11.2 N/kg
at sea level standard conditions.
Pressure Vessel Mass vs. Payload Budget (NEW)
HTC operates at autogenous pressure (10–40 bar). Reactor vessel wall
thickness is governed by hoop stress: t = P × r / (σ_allowable × η_weld)
Thicker walls mean heavier vessels, which consume more of the payload
budget. This creates a direct coupling between operating temperature
(which sets pressure), vessel mass, and the number of modules an airship
can carry. Higher temperature improves reaction kinetics but increases
pressure, increases wall thickness, and reduces module count.



ANALYSIS DOMAINS AND GOVERNING EQUATIONS


These four analysis modules have been scoped and defined. They are the
analytical core of the project. Python computational tools will implement
each module and then integrate them in Module 5.
--- Module 1: Payload & Buoyancy Feasibility ---
Primary equations:
Buoyant force:   F_b = (ρ_air - ρ_lift_gas) × V_envelope × g
Net lift:        F_net = F_b - M_airship_empty × g
Payload budget:  M_payload = F_net / g
Module count:    N_modules = floor(M_payload / M_per_module)
Hydrogen offtake coupling:
ΔM_H2_vented = mass of hydrogen vented after module deployment
ΔF_b_lost = ΔM_H2_vented × (ρ_air - ρ_H2) × g
E_H2_gained = ΔM_H2_vented × LHV_H2
Inputs:  Envelope volume (m³), lift gas identity (H2 or He), airship empty
mass (kg), module mass estimate (kg/module)
Outputs: Maximum module count N; feasible throughput range (kg/hr);
hydrogen offtake energy available vs. buoyancy cost curve
Key assumption to verify: Airship platform specification (envelope volume,
empty mass, rated payload). Use a published platform — HAV 304,
Lockheed LMH-1, or similar — not a hypothetical.
--- Module 2: Reactor Thermal Energy Balance ---
Primary equation:
Q_net = Q_reaction - Q_loss - Q_preheat_net
Q_reaction    = m_dot × HHV_feedstock × η_HTC
Q_loss        = U × A_reactor × (T_reactor - T_ambient)
Q_preheat     = m_dot × moisture_fraction × c_p_water × (T_reactor - T_feed)
Q_preheat_net = Q_preheat × (1 - ε)      [after heat exchanger recovery]
Inputs:  m_dot (from Module 1), HHV and η_HTC (from literature per feedstock),
moisture_fraction (swept 0.40–0.80), T_reactor (design parameter,
180–250°C), U (estimated W/m²·K), A_reactor (from Module 3 geometry),
ε (from Module 4), T_ambient, T_feed
Outputs: Q_net (kW) as function of moisture and throughput; crossover
moisture threshold (Q_net = 0 boundary); minimum η_HTC for
self-sufficiency at each moisture level
Key parameters to obtain from literature before coding:
HHV (kJ/kg dry) and η_HTC for municipal food waste and sewage sludge
--- Module 3: Pressure Vessel Mechanical Analysis (Hoop Stress) ---
Primary equations:
Hoop stress:      σ_hoop = P × r / t
Required thickness: t = P × r / (σ_allowable × η_weld)
Vessel mass:      M_vessel = ρ_steel × 2π × r × L × t  (cylindrical shell)
End caps add approximately 15–20% to shell mass (first-pass estimate)
Where:
P = autogenous pressure at T_reactor (from steam tables, Pa)
r = inner radius of vessel (m), a design parameter
σ_allowable = yield strength / safety factor (Pa); for 316 SS,
σ_yield ≈ 205 MPa; apply safety factor of 3–4 for
pressure vessel service → σ_allowable ≈ 50–68 MPa
η_weld = weld efficiency factor, typically 0.85 for radiographed welds
ρ_steel ≈ 8000 kg/m³
Inputs:  T_reactor (sets P via steam tables), vessel geometry (r, L),
material selection (σ_yield, ρ), safety factor, weld efficiency
Outputs: Wall thickness t (mm); vessel mass M_vessel (kg); M_vessel as
function of T_reactor; how vessel mass changes module count
(feeds back into Module 1)
Key tension: Higher T_reactor → higher P → thicker walls → heavier vessel
→ fewer modules per airship. This coupling must be explicit.
Design decision produced: Operating temperature that balances reaction
kinetics benefit against structural mass penalty.
--- Module 4: Heat Exchanger Effectiveness ---
Primary equations (ε-NTU method):
ε = Q_actual / Q_max
Q_max = C_min × (T_hot_in - T_cold_in)
C = m_dot × c_p   for each stream
C_min = min(C_hot, C_cold)
Q_recovered = ε × C_min × (T_hot_in - T_cold_in)
Inputs:  ε (design parameter, swept 0.0–0.85), T_hot_in (reactor outlet,
~T_reactor), T_cold_in (feed temperature, ~15–30°C), m_dot,
c_p for each stream (~4.18 kJ/kg·K for water-dominant streams)
Outputs: Q_recovered (kW); minimum ε to close energy balance; sensitivity
of Q_net to ε degradation (fouling scenario); Q_net at ε=0
(no-recovery baseline) vs. ε=0.70 (realistic)
Assumption: Steady-state counterflow, no phase change in HX, constant
flow rates. Fouling neglected at this analysis stage.
--- Module 5: Self-Sufficiency Integration ---
Governing inequality (assembled from Modules 2, 3, 4):
Q_net(moisture, m_dot, T_reactor, ε) >= W_pump + W_auxiliary
Pump work (from slurry mechanics):
W_pump = m_dot × ΔP / (ρ_slurry × η_pump)
ΔP dominated by P_autogenous(T_reactor) — pressurization cost
Darcy-Weisbach for friction: ΔP_f = f × (L/D) × (ρ × v² / 2)
η_pump = 0.65 (initial estimate for slurry service)
ρ_slurry ≈ 950–1100 kg/m³ (function of moisture and solids density)
W_auxiliary: estimated at 5–10% of Q_net as placeholder for instrumentation,
controls, communications load
Output: 2D feasibility surface in {moisture content × throughput} space
showing: (1) the self-sufficient operating region, (2) the
boundary Q_net = W_pump + W_auxiliary, (3) sensitivity to ε
and T_reactor as parameter shifts.
This surface is the primary technical deliverable of the project.


DISASTER OPERATIONS CONSTRAINTS


These are not captured by the engineering equations above but directly
constrain system design and operational parameters. Each must be addressed
in the final specification or flagged as a scope boundary.
Deployment Environment Constraints:

No fixed infrastructure: no grid power, no piped water, no roads for
heavy equipment. All energy and water must be self-supplied or
extracted from the waste stream.
Terrain uncertainty: landing zones may be uneven, unstable, or
partially flooded. Reactor modules must be level-tolerant or include
leveling mechanisms.
Airspace coordination: airship operations in disaster zones will
conflict with helicopters and fixed-wing relief aircraft. Operational
altitude and loiter time are not freely chosen design parameters —
they are negotiated with incident command.

Waste Stream Uncertainty:

Disaster waste is heterogeneous and unpredictable. A single feedstock
assumption is not realistic. The system must tolerate a range of
moisture content and organic fraction without manual reconfiguration.
Feedstock characterization on-site is limited. Operating logic should
be robust to measurement uncertainty, not dependent on precise feedstock
composition data.

Operational Tempo:

Deployment must be achievable within hours of arrival, not days.
System complexity (connections, calibration, startup) must be minimized.
Operators in disaster zones are not process engineers. Interfaces must
be simple; failure modes must be safe and obvious.
Modules must be recoverable and redeployable. Design for disassembly
under field conditions.

Safety and Regulatory Constraints:

HTC at 10–40 bar in a field environment is a significant pressure
hazard. Pressure relief systems, burst disks, and safe venting pathways
are non-negotiable design elements.
Hydrogen use as a supplemental fuel introduces flammability risk
adjacent to airship operations. This interaction requires explicit
hazard analysis (deferred to later phase).
No current regulatory framework exists for airborne chemical processing.
The system likely falls under multiple overlapping jurisdictions
(aviation, chemical processing, disaster response). Flag as a major
project risk.

Logistics and Resupply:

The system must operate without resupply for a defined mission duration
(72 hours minimum as a design target — to be verified).
Consumables (pump seals, filter media, instrumentation) must be
minimized. Process water recirculation reduces fresh water demand but
requires treatment to prevent buildup of dissolved solids.



SYSTEMS MAP — MODULE COUPLING LOGIC


Payload Analysis (Module 1)
→ Produces: feasible throughput range m_dot; maximum module count N
→ Feeds into: all modules (m_dot is the common thread)
→ Receives from: Module 3 (vessel mass affects module mass and count)
Note: Module 1 and Module 3 are mutually dependent and must be
solved iteratively or simultaneously.
Reactor Thermal Balance (Module 2)
→ Produces: Q_net as function of {moisture, m_dot, T_reactor, ε}
→ Feeds into: Module 5 (Q_net is the left-hand side of self-sufficiency)
→ Receives from: Module 1 (m_dot), Module 3 (T_reactor → pressure coupling),
Module 4 (ε → Q_preheat_net)
Pressure Vessel Mechanics (Module 3)
→ Produces: wall thickness t(T_reactor), vessel mass M_vessel(T_reactor)
→ Feeds into: Module 1 (vessel mass updates module mass budget)
Module 2 (T_reactor is shared input; pressure is derived here)
Note: T_reactor is a design parameter set once and shared across
Modules 2, 3, and 5. Sweeping T_reactor propagates through all three.
Heat Exchanger Effectiveness (Module 4)
→ Produces: Q_recovered(ε); minimum ε required; Q_preheat_net
→ Feeds into: Module 2 (reduces Q_preheat term)
Note: Modules 2 and 4 are coupled and solved iteratively if ε is not
fixed. Recommended approach: fix ε as a parameter, sweep it
externally across Module 5.
Self-Sufficiency Integration (Module 5)
→ Assembles: Q_net from Module 2, W_pump from slurry mechanics,
W_auxiliary (fixed estimate)
→ Produces: feasibility surface in {moisture × m_dot} space;
sensitivity maps for T_reactor and ε
→ This is the terminal module. Its output is the primary deliverable.
Shared Variables (appear in multiple modules — must be defined once):
T_reactor:       design parameter; shared by Modules 2, 3, 5
m_dot:           from Module 1; shared by Modules 2, 4, 5
moisture_fraction: swept input; shared by Modules 2, 5
ε:               design parameter; shared by Modules 4, 2, 5
P_autogenous:    derived from T_reactor via steam tables; used in Modules 3, 5

PYTHON IMPLEMENTATION PLAN


Sequence: Implement modules in order 3 → 1 → 4 → 2 → 5.
Rationale: Module 3 (hoop stress) is algebraically simple and produces
vessel mass needed by Module 1. Module 1 produces m_dot range. Module 4
is independent of m_dot in its core calculation. Module 2 uses all prior
outputs. Module 5 assembles everything.
Each module should be implemented as:

A standalone Python function with clearly named arguments matching the
variable names in this document
A sweep loop that evaluates the function across the parameter range
specified in the specification table
A simple plot output (matplotlib) showing the swept result
A printed summary of the key design decision produced

Steam table data for P_autogenous: implement as a curve-fit polynomial
or a small lookup dictionary — do not use external thermodynamic libraries
at this stage (keep dependencies minimal for a Python beginner).
All physical constants and literature values must be defined in a single
constants block at the top of each script, with source annotations.
No magic numbers embedded in equations.
