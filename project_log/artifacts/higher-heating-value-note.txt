The Higher Heating Value (HHV), or gross calorific value, is
the total thermal energy released during complete fuel combustion, including latent heat from condensing water vapor in the exhaust back to liquid at
. It represents the maximum potential energy available, commonly used in North American engineering. 
Key Aspects of Higher Heating Value (HHV):

    Definition: Also known as gross energy, upper heating value, or gross calorific value (GCV).
    Water Condensation: HHV assumes all water produced during combustion is condensed into a liquid, capturing the latent heat of vaporization.
    Common Applications: Primarily used in North America to calculate boiler efficiency and for fuels where water vapor condensation is practical (e.g., condensing boilers).
    Compared to LHV: HHV is higher than the Lower Heating Value (LHV) because it includes this latent heat, whereas LHV treats it as lost energy.
    Measurement: Determined via a bomb calorimeter (ASTM standards). 

Common Fuel HHV Examples (Approximate):  (Mj/kg)
   
    Natural Gas: 42.5
    Diesel: 45.9
    Gasoline: 46.7
    Coal: 34.1
    Hydrogen: 141.9
